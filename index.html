1) What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

Answer

Almost instantly as I entered the webpage, the colours felt distorting and bright as I naturally dragged my mouse around to explore and discover. I quickly aligned my mouse with the margins of the text so that I could read it clearly, and the major influences of the mouse soon became a distraction.

2) Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

Answer

I focused very intently on the mouse’s interactions and movements, wondering to myself how these interactions were put into place. I spent most of my time on the ‘pixel displacement effect’, the waves generated by the movements of the mouse is very satisfying to the eyes. I tried to move my mouse aggressively across the screen as well as slowly sliding from one side to another to see the difference in appearance and reaction.

3) What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

Answer

As mentioned above, the ‘pixel displacement effect’ caught my attention for majority of the 2 minutes I was on that webpage. Other than that, I final page was also quite amusing, the use of hover over text quite appealing due to the way each individual character moves on its own.

4) What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

Answer

Dragging mouse around more, both calmly and aggressively. Testing the visual reactions of the webpage as many as ways as I could think of at the time due to the ‘lack of clicking’ required to navigate between sections.

5) What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

Answer

General impressions and first glance thoughts were a webpage with conventional interactive elements such as buttons, switches, and unique widgets. However, I was greeted with mouse intensive responses combined with ‘fidgety’ elements. The initial intention was to reach the bottom of the webpage, but as I was greeted with interactions, I spent longer on each page.

6) How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

Answer

With the lack of conventional buttons, widgets, sliders, and menus, the webpage locked the user (myself) in an experience that was targeted on mouse movements. With each movement, subtle or large, it would urge me to continue to experiment and explore the limitations of how the aspects would move and change.

7) What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

Answer

I suppose 2 minutes was a reasonable amount of time a user would spend on the webpage. Over the course of 2 times would be best, for the user to reflect and compare experiences for the first and second occasions. The colours and the way each interaction moves feels very ‘ocd’ soothing, which may target better at low attention span users, the interaction spanning for under 2 minutes, but still over the course of 2-3 times/interactions.

8) How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

Answer

As mentioned above, the use of colours and the way each interactive experience is designed to be highly responsive to the user indicates that it only takes 2-3 interactions for it to be realised and accepted. The experience should gradually become more intentional over time, especially when a user has interacted with similar interfaces in the past to make actions more intentional. A small portion of the exploration done comes from a previously felt experience to make each interaction progressively more shifted away from instinct and more towards intended use.

9) What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

Answer

Games, fidgets, distractions, short form content, and animations. Mainly fidgets and representations, almost ‘gamified’ mix with the portfolio page aspect in mind.

10) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

Answer

Acting indecisively and freely, especially for the first time shows how users like myself explores with the webpage. The depth and no filter of just simply enjoying and interacting with its limits. This allows primary sensory data to be gathered first hand without too much outside influences. Intentional uncertainty is the key references towards a meaningful research experience.

11) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

Answer

Visual aspects allow further analysis on how we behave towards digital designs and shape our expectations of future models. Neutral and passive to begin with and then letting the fluidity of the moment to carry my behaviours and emotions onwards. Expected feelings would include satisfaction and ‘the hopes of finding something new and interesting’. This is what continues to stimulate and prompt us to explore.

12) What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

Answer

There was no natural frustration in the moment, however revisiting similar aspects along side pausing to understand and reflect on the visuals felt repetitive. This hints frustration in a way that urges impatience during the experience.

13) What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

Answer

Definitely the ‘pixel displacement effect’, and the final page coming in second. I personally couldn’t get enough of how the effects distort, the speed and reception of the interactions, and just the simplicity of the texture displayed on the screen. The final page was also very appealing in the way the texts changes when hovered, creating a very compelling effect for the user to play around and repeat the process.
